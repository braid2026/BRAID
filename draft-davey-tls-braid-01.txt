  Bound Routing, Authority, and Identity Data (BRAID): DNSSEC-Anchored
              Authorization for TLS Delegated Credentials
                        draft-davey-tls-braid-01

Abstract

   This document defines the BRAID Identity strand, a negotiated TLS
   profile that augments Delegated Credentials (RFC 9345) with owner-
   controlled, DNSSEC-authenticated key authorization.  A BRAID server
   authenticates a TLS connection with a Delegated Credential.  A BRAID
   client accepts that credential only if it validates under RFC 9345
   and the credential's public key is listed in a DNSSEC-authenticated
   BRAID Anchor published by the domain owner.  This creates a two-
   control use condition: possession of the end-entity private key is
   insufficient to impersonate a protected domain unless the attacker
   can also publish an authorized credential key in the owner's DNSSEC-
   signed zone.  The property holds even when the Anchor and the
   credential key are static; freshness parameters are an optional
   enforcement profile that bounds revocation latency and stolen-
   credential-key exposure, and are not the source of the security
   property.  Support is negotiated in the handshake, so non-BRAID
   clients receive conventional certificates and are unaffected.  The
   document additionally defines a monitor-only deployment mode that
   runs entirely on infrastructure deployed today, explicit DNSSEC
   validation modes that state the trust model precisely, and optional
   future extension profiles for routing and witness assurance.  It
   does not define or change maximum validity periods for publicly
   trusted certificates; any longer-lived certificate profile would
   require separate root-program action supported by deployment
   evidence.

1.  Introduction

   Certificate revocation for publicly trusted TLS certificates has
   never worked reliably at internet scale.  Online status checks
   (OCSP) have been deprecated by major browsers for privacy, latency,
   and soft-fail reasons; certificate revocation lists are not
   consulted in a way that protects users; and the ecosystem's
   response, progressively shorter certificate lifetimes, mitigates
   the exposure window of a bad certificate without repairing the
   underlying mechanism.

   The -00 revision of this document attempted to address the lifetime
   question and the revocation question together.  Review made clear
   that the two are separable, and that the durable contribution is
   narrower and stronger than the original framing suggested.  This
   revision therefore isolates one primitive and builds the document
   around it:

      A domain owner publishes, under DNSSEC, the set of Delegated
      Credential public keys currently authorized to authenticate its
      TLS service.  A client that enforces this check requires both a
      valid RFC 9345 delegation from the WebPKI end-entity certificate
      and the owner's Anchor authorization of the credential key.
      Impersonation therefore requires compromising two independently
      held controls: the end-entity private key and the owner's DNS
      publication path.

   This is the two-control property (Section 4.1).  It is a
   consequence of authorization membership, not of freshness: it holds
   even when the Anchor is published once and never changed, and even
   when the operator reuses the same credential key indefinitely.
   Freshness parameters remain available as an optional enforcement
   profile (Section 12.4) because they bound two narrower risks --- the
   useful lifetime of a stolen credential key and the propagation
   latency of a revocation --- but they are no longer the foundation of
   the design, and the baseline profile imposes no recurring DNS-side
   or CA-facing operations (Section 13.1).

   BRAID does not claim to eliminate automation.  It changes what must
   be automated and who controls the failure domain.  Conventional
   short-lived certificates require repeated public issuance events:
   CA interaction, domain-control validation, rate limits, profile
   compliance, and CT logging, on an external party's schedule,
   availability, and commercial terms.  Under BRAID-Base, the
   security-bearing artifacts --- the Anchor RRset and the credential
   key --- are static; the only recurring operation is re-signing the
   Delegated Credential locally with the end-entity key the operator
   already holds, an operation with no external dependency, no domain
   validation, and no DNS write, whose failure is visible to the
   operator's own monitoring before it affects anyone (Section 13.1).

   Two design principles carry over from -00.  First, reuse over
   reinvention: BRAID composes Delegated Credentials [RFC9345], the
   TLS Feature extension [RFC7633], Subject Information Access and
   GeneralSubtrees [RFC5280], and DNSSEC, each with deployed parsers
   and operational history; the new parsing surface is a single short
   extension sequence and one resource record.  Second, keep expensive
   validation off the client's critical path and state the trust model
   precisely: this revision replaces the -00 resolver story with two
   explicit DNSSEC validation modes (Section 9), so that a verifier
   always knows, and a reviewer can always determine, exactly what is
   trusted.

   BRAID changes nothing for parties that do not adopt it.  Support is
   negotiated (Section 10): a server presents a BRAID certificate only
   to a client that has offered to validate it, and presents a
   conventional certificate to all others.  Deployment is staged
   (Section 12), beginning with a monitor-only mode that requires no
   new client, CA, TLS, or root-program change and is independently
   useful on day one.

2.  Non-Goals

   Stating what this document does not attempt is part of its design.
   BRAID-Base:

   *  does not replace WebPKI certificate validation; the Authority
      strand (the CA-signed certificate) remains required;

   *  does not remove the need for conventional certificates while
      non-BRAID clients exist, and does not remove the operator's
      existing renewal automation for those certificates;

   *  does not mandate in-band DNSSEC validation by general-purpose
      browsers; the validation modes of Section 9 state exactly what
      each deployment class trusts;

   *  does not define, request, or imply any change to maximum
      validity periods for publicly trusted certificates, which are
      set by root-program policy and the CA/Browser Forum, not the
      IETF (Section 14);

   *  does not prevent impersonation by an attacker who simultaneously
      compromises both the end-entity private key and the owner's DNS
      publication path; and

   *  does not require routing-origin validation, witness co-signing,
      revocation transparency, or private-use gating; these are
      extension profiles (Section 19) and are not prerequisites for
      implementing or deploying anything in this document.

3.  Terminology

   Anchor (BRAID Anchor):  A DNSSEC-signed RRset, published by the
      domain owner, listing the Delegated Credential public keys (by
      hash) currently authorized to authenticate TLS for a DNS-ID.

   Anchor entry:  One authorized-key record within the Anchor RRset.

   Credential key:  The public/private key pair whose public half is
      carried in a Delegated Credential and whose private half signs
      the TLS handshake.

   DNS-ID:  A DNS domain name identity presented in a certificate's
      subjectAltName extension, per [RFC6125].

   End-entity (EE) key:  The subject key pair of the server's CA-
      issued certificate; under [RFC9345] the EE private key signs
      Delegated Credentials.

   Identity strand:  The owner-controlled authorization layer defined
      by this document: an RFC 9345 Delegated Credential whose public
      key is authorized by the Anchor.

   Authority strand:  The CA-signed X.509 certificate, validated under
      normal WebPKI rules [RFC5280], logged to Certificate
      Transparency.

   Two-control property:  The property that impersonation of a
      protected domain requires both the EE private key and write
      access to the owner's DNSSEC-signed zone (Section 4.1).  (The
      -00 revision called this the "two-key property".)

   BRAID-Base:  The baseline profile: Authority strand plus Identity
      strand with membership checking only; static Anchor and static
      credential key permitted.

   BRAID-Agile:  The optional freshness enforcement profile
      (Section 12.4).

4.  Security Model

4.1.  The two-control property

   The Identity strand is an owner-controlled authorization layer for
   the TLS credential key used by the server.  It composes RFC 9345
   Delegated Credentials with a DNSSEC-authenticated BRAID Anchor.
   The Delegated Credential proves that the end-entity certificate key
   delegated authority to a credential key; the Anchor proves that the
   domain owner currently authorizes that credential key for use.  The
   Identity strand validates only when both statements are true.

   The resulting security property is independent of freshness
   cadence.  An adversary who steals the end-entity private key can
   mint syntactically valid Delegated Credentials --- [RFC9345] alone
   cannot prevent that, since the end-entity key is the delegator.
   But those credentials name adversary-chosen public keys, and the
   adversary cannot cause those keys to appear in the owner's DNSSEC-
   signed Anchor RRset without separately compromising the owner's DNS
   publication path.  Conversely, an adversary who compromises the
   owner's zone can publish Anchor entries but holds no CA-signed
   certificate and no end-entity key with which to mint a credential
   those entries would authorize.  Impersonation therefore requires
   the simultaneous compromise of two independently held and
   independently operated controls:

      (1) the end-entity private key, and
      (2) write access to the owner's DNSSEC-signed zone.

   Crucially, this holds even when the Anchor RRset and the credential
   key are completely static.  A long-lived credential key that the
   thief does not possess is as useless to the thief as a short-lived
   one; what defeats the thief is that the client checks membership of
   the handshake key in the owner's Anchor, not that the key changes
   often.  Freshness parameters (Section 12.4) bound two narrower and
   genuinely distinct risks: how long a stolen *credential* key
   remains usable before its Anchor entry or credential interval
   lapses, and how quickly an owner-initiated de-authorization
   propagates globally.  Both are valuable; neither is the source of
   the two-control property.

   To preserve the independence of the two controls in practice, the
   DNS update credential used to publish the Anchor SHOULD NOT be
   resident on the TLS-terminating hosts that hold the end-entity or
   credential keys, and the zone's DNSSEC signing key SHOULD be
   operated per current best practice (offline or HSM-held where
   feasible).  Section 15 analyzes the collapse of the property when
   this separation is not maintained.

5.  Comparison with DANE TLSA

   The closest existing mechanism is DANE TLSA [RFC6698], and the
   comparison deserves precision, because a superficial reading makes
   the two appear equivalent: both publish key material in a DNSSEC-
   signed zone and both give the owner a record they can withdraw.
   The difference is in what object the record vouches for, and it is
   decisive in the stolen-key case.

   TLSA certificate usage 1 (PKIX-EE) binds the DNSSEC-authenticated
   name to the end-entity certificate or its public key, in addition
   to normal PKIX validation.  This is valuable against mis-issuance:
   a certificate issued by another CA for the same name fails the TLSA
   match.  But it does not, by itself, prevent use of a stolen end-
   entity private key: the attacker presents the same certificate and
   proves possession of the same key that the TLSA record
   authenticates.  The record vouches for exactly the object that was
   stolen, so the check passes.  The same analysis applies to usage 3
   (DANE-EE).

   BRAID authenticates a different object.  The Anchor authorizes the
   public key of the Delegated Credential permitted to authenticate
   the connection --- a key one delegation step removed from the end-
   entity key.  A stolen end-entity key lets the attacker mint a
   syntactically valid credential, but does not let the attacker place
   that credential's public key into the owner's Anchor.  A strict
   Identity verifier therefore rejects the attacker-minted credential.
   This is the two-control property, and TLSA cannot express it,
   because TLSA has no vocabulary for authorizing a key other than the
   one in (or matching) the presented certificate.

   The revocation semantics also differ in a way that matters
   operationally.  Withdrawing a TLSA usage 1 record to "revoke"
   affects the legitimate service and the attacker identically ---
   both present the same certificate and key --- so revocation-by-
   withdrawal is also self-inflicted denial of service, and it is only
   meaningful at all to clients that treat the record's absence as
   fatal, which requires a separate signal that the domain requires
   TLSA (otherwise absence simply means "no TLSA deployed" and clients
   fall back to ordinary PKIX acceptance).  Under BRAID, the
   requirement signal is signed into the certificate itself as a
   critical extension (Section 6.2), so absence of authorization is
   fatal by construction for BRAID-negotiated connections; and
   revocation is selective: the owner removes the compromised
   credential key's Anchor entry while the service continues on a
   different authorized key, de-authorizing the attacker without an
   outage.

   Finally, this document does not treat the limited deployment of
   TLSA for the public web as evidence against BRAID's deployability,
   for two reasons that the design reflects.  First, TLSA's marginal
   security value over WebPKI validation is contested precisely
   because of the stolen-key analysis above; BRAID's marginal value is
   the two-control property, which is a different and demonstrably
   stronger claim.  Second, TLSA-for-web foundered on in-band client
   DNSSEC validation with an implicit trust model; this document makes
   the trust model explicit and modal (Section 9) and defines a
   deployment mode requiring no client changes at all (Section 12.1).

6.  The Identity Strand

6.1.  Construction

   The mechanism composes three parts, of which only the third is new:

   1.  *The Delegated Credential, per [RFC9345] unchanged.*  The
       operator mints a credential --- a public key and a validity
       interval, which [RFC9345] caps at seven days from issuance ---
       signed by the end-entity certificate's private key, exactly as
       [RFC9345] specifies.  The server signs the handshake with the
       credential's private key.  Note that the seven-day cap bounds
       the credential *object*, not the credential *key*: an operator
       MAY re-sign successive credentials over the same key pair, in
       which case the Anchor never changes (Section 13.1).

   2.  *The BRAID Anchor.*  The domain owner publishes, in its DNSSEC-
       signed zone at the name determined by Section 7.1, an Anchor
       RRset listing the credential public keys (by hash) authorized
       to serve the DNS-ID.  The RRset MAY list several keys to permit
       overlap during rotation.  Under BRAID-Base the RRset is
       published once and changed only to rotate or revoke a key.

   3.  *The client's conjunctive check.*  A client enforcing the
       Identity strand MUST verify all of: (a) the Delegated
       Credential validates under [RFC9345] against the end-entity
       certificate; (b) the handshake was authenticated by the
       credential's key; and (c) the hash of the credential's public
       key appears in the DNSSEC-authenticated Anchor RRset applicable
       to the validated DNS-ID.  Failure of any check fails the
       strand.  The complete algorithm, including freshness and name-
       constraint steps, is given in Section 8.

6.2.  The BRAIDBinding certificate extension

   A BRAID certificate carries a critical X.509 extension,
   BRAIDBinding, declaring that the Identity strand is REQUIRED and
   carrying the minimal parameters needed to locate and verify it.
   Because the extension is critical [RFC5280], a client that does not
   implement BRAID rejects the certificate rather than treating it as
   an ordinary certificate; this prevents silent downgrade to single-
   control validation.  Presentation to non-BRAID clients is prevented
   by the negotiation of Section 10, so criticality never strands a
   legacy client.

   BRAIDBinding contains:

   *  a strand requirement indicator; in this document only the
      Identity strand is defined as REQUIRABLE (the Authority strand
      is always present; Routing and Witness indicators are reserved
      for the extension profiles of Section 19);

   *  OPTIONALLY an Anchor-name override, constrained by the rules of
      Section 7.1;

   *  OPTIONALLY the freshness parameters of the BRAID-Agile profile
      (Section 12.4): a maximum acceptable credential age, not to
      exceed the [RFC9345] seven-day maximum;

   *  OPTIONALLY the delegated name constraints of Section 6.3,
      expressed in the GeneralSubtrees syntax of [RFC5280]; and

   *  the policy mode under which the certificate was issued
      (Section 12).

   In addition, the certificate SHOULD carry the TLS Feature extension
   [RFC7633] listing the braid_chain ExtensionType, declaring in the
   long-standing "must-staple" idiom that the handshake is required to
   include the BRAID material; this gives middleware and audit tooling
   that already parses [RFC7633] visibility into the requirement
   without new code.  Locators (the URI of an optional proof
   repository) are carried as GeneralName access descriptors in the
   Subject Information Access extension [RFC5280].  The precise ASN.1
   module is deferred to a companion LAMPS document (Appendix B); the
   new parsing surface is deliberately a single short sequence.

6.3.  Delegated name constraints

   A Delegated Credential inherits the full name scope of the end-
   entity certificate that signs it: [RFC9345] provides no means to
   limit which of the certificate's identities a given credential may
   assert.  Where credentials are handed to a third party such as a
   CDN edge, this over-authorizes the holder: a compromised edge
   credential can impersonate any name on the certificate, including
   sensitive subdomains.  Because X.509 Name Constraints [RFC5280] are
   issued by CAs and do not apply to Delegated Credentials, BRAID
   closes the gap at the policy layer: the delegated name constraints
   carried in BRAIDBinding enumerate the identities a credential is
   permitted to assert.  A client enforcing the Identity strand MUST
   reject a Delegated Credential whose asserted identity falls outside
   this set.

   Matching follows [RFC6125], with the following precision so that no
   edge ambiguity survives in multi-tenant deployments: comparison is
   performed on lowercase A-labels (the Punycode form for
   internationalized names); a wildcard is permitted only as the
   entire left-most label; partial-label wildcards, including any
   wildcard within an A-label such as xn--*, MUST NOT be accepted; a
   wildcard never matches the apex or a multi-label suffix; and a name
   that is syntactically malformed under these rules fails the
   Identity strand rather than being repaired or partially matched.
   An empty or absent constraint set means the credential inherits the
   certificate's full name scope, exactly as under [RFC9345] today, so
   the field is strictly additive.  In combination with per-edge
   Anchor entries --- each edge's credential key listed separately, so
   any one edge can be de-authorized by removing one entry --- this
   makes third-party serving deployments materially safer at no added
   handshake cost.

7.  The BRAID Anchor

7.1.  Anchor location

   The -00 revision located the Anchor "at the name given in the
   certificate", which is too loose: it invites ambiguity around
   aliasing, cross-domain pointers, CNAME chains, public suffixes, and
   who may publish for whom.  This revision replaces it with a
   deterministic rule.

   For each DNS-ID validated in the certificate, the default Anchor
   name is:

      _braid.<DNS-ID>

   A BRAIDBinding MAY specify a different Anchor name only if that
   name is beneath the same DNS-ID or beneath the same registrable
   domain, and the delegation path to that name is DNSSEC-
   authenticated.  A BRAID certificate MUST NOT rely on an Anchor
   outside the authenticated DNS identity boundary unless an explicit
   DNSSEC-authenticated indirection record published at the default
   Anchor name authorizes that external Anchor.  CNAME chasing alone
   is NOT sufficient to authorize an external Anchor: an unsigned or
   merely-followed alias does not transfer authorization authority.

7.2.  Multi-SAN and wildcard semantics

   When a certificate contains multiple DNS-IDs, a client MUST
   validate the Anchor applicable to the DNS-ID used for [RFC6125]
   name validation on this connection.  Authorization for one DNS-ID
   does not authorize another DNS-ID in the same certificate unless
   the Anchor entry explicitly identifies the covered DNS-ID set and
   that set is within the certificate's subjectAltName extension.

   For a wildcard DNS-ID (*.example.com), the default Anchor name is
   formed from the parent domain (_braid.example.com), and an Anchor
   entry covering a wildcard DNS-ID applies to exactly the names the
   wildcard matches under [RFC6125]; it does not cover the apex.

7.3.  TXT transition form

   Until the BRAID Anchor RRTYPE (Section 18) is widely supported by
   provisioning systems, the RRset MAY equivalently be published as a
   TXT record at the same _braid-prefixed name.  SPF, DKIM, and DMARC
   each launched on TXT records and demonstrated that this path
   carries production traffic at internet scale.  A validator MUST
   treat a DNSSEC-validated TXT-form Anchor as equivalent to the
   native RRTYPE during the transition; all authentication and
   location rules of this section apply identically to the TXT form.

7.4.  Anchor content

   Each Anchor entry contains: a hash algorithm identifier; the hash
   of an authorized credential public key, computed over the
   credential's canonical public-key encoding; OPTIONALLY a covered
   DNS-ID set (Section 7.2); and OPTIONALLY an entry validity interval
   used by the BRAID-Agile profile (Section 12.4).  Under BRAID-Base,
   entries without a validity interval are valid while published.
   The wire format of the RRTYPE and the canonical key encoding are
   deferred to the companion DNSOP document (Appendix B).

8.  Validation Algorithm

   Prose-only validation invites corner cases; the normative check is
   therefore stated as an algorithm.  A client enforcing the Identity
   strand executes IdentityValidate and MUST fail closed on any
   missing, malformed, stale, or ambiguous input.

   IdentityValidate(cert, dc, handshake, anchor_rrset, policy):

     1.  Verify cert under normal WebPKI rules [RFC5280], including
         [RFC6125] name validation of the DNS-ID for this connection.
     2.  Verify cert contains a critical BRAIDBinding extension
         requiring the Identity strand.
     3.  Verify dc is a valid [RFC9345] Delegated Credential for
         cert, including its validity interval.
     4.  Verify the TLS handshake was authenticated by dc.public_key.
     5.  Canonicalize dc.public_key per the Anchor hash algorithm.
     6.  Verify anchor_rrset is DNSSEC-authenticated under the trust
         mode required by policy (Section 9).
     7.  Verify anchor_rrset's name is authorized for the validated
         DNS-ID under the location rules of Section 7.1 and the
         multi-SAN rules of Section 7.2.
     8.  Verify Hash(dc.public_key) appears in anchor_rrset, and that
         the matching entry covers the validated DNS-ID.
     9.  If freshness is required by cert or by relying-party policy
         (Section 12.4), verify the dc validity interval and the
         Anchor entry validity interval satisfy that policy.
     10. If delegated name constraints are present (Section 6.3),
         verify the asserted DNS-ID against them.
     11. On any failure, abort the handshake; no other strand or
         mechanism compensates for an Identity failure in strict
         mode (Section 12.3).

9.  DNSSEC Validation Modes

   A frequent and fair criticism of DNS-anchored TLS mechanisms is
   that "the client validates DNSSEC" conceals several very different
   trust models, some of which (trusting an unauthenticated resolver's
   AD bit across an open network) are not security mechanisms at all.
   A BRAID verifier MUST therefore know which trust mode it operates
   in, and a certificate or policy that requires one mode MUST NOT be
   satisfied by a weaker one.

   *Public-Web strict mode.*  The verifier validates the DNSSEC proof
   chain for the Anchor RRset itself, using material that is self-
   authenticating end to end: cached chains, chains fetched by
   reference (Section 11), material stapled by the server, or an
   authenticated snapshot distributed with the client software (in the
   manner of preloaded HSTS lists and CRLite-style revocation
   snapshots, which demonstrate that browser-distributed authenticated
   data sets are an operational reality today).  The verifier MUST NOT
   treat an unauthenticated resolver response, including an
   unauthenticated AD bit, as sufficient in this mode.

   *Managed-Resolver mode.*  Enterprise, mobile-operator, or managed-
   endpoint deployments MAY rely on an authenticated, encrypted
   channel (such as DNS over TLS [RFC7858] or DNS over HTTPS
   [RFC8484]) to a validating resolver operated under the relying
   party's own administrative policy.  This mode trades cryptographic
   self-sufficiency for operational simplicity and is appropriate
   exactly where the resolver is part of the relying party's trusted
   computing base.

   This split does not make public-web deployment easy; it makes the
   trust model honest.  The monitor-only mode of Section 12.1 requires
   neither, because no client behavior changes at all; managed
   environments (Section 12.2) have Managed-Resolver mode available
   today; and the Public-Web strict mode is gated behind the
   deployment evidence of Section 12.3 rather than assumed.

10.  Negotiation and Certificate Selection

   BRAID requires TLS 1.3 or later [RFC8446]; the Identity strand
   inherits this floor from Delegated Credentials [RFC9345].  Because
   BRAIDBinding is critical, a non-BRAID client would reject a BRAID
   certificate; BRAID therefore MUST be negotiated, and a server MUST
   NOT present a BRAID certificate to a client that has not offered to
   validate it.

   *Client signal.*  A BRAID-capable client offers the braid_chain
   extension (Section 11) in its ClientHello, alongside the
   delegated_credential extension of [RFC9345].  The presence of
   braid_chain in the ClientHello is the client's declaration that it
   implements this document and will enforce the certificate's
   required strands.

   *Server certificate selection.*  A server operating a BRAID
   deployment holds both a BRAID certificate and a conventional
   certificate for the same identity.  If the ClientHello offers
   braid_chain, the server SHOULD select the BRAID certificate and
   respond with the braid_chain extension; otherwise it MUST select
   the conventional certificate and complete an ordinary TLS 1.3
   handshake.  This mirrors the certificate-selection model already
   deployed for Delegated Credentials and signature-algorithm
   negotiation; BRAID adoption is invisible to every legacy client.

   *Operational isolation.*  A BRAID certificate MUST NOT be
   configured as the default or fallback certificate of any listener;
   it is selected only on an affirmative braid_chain offer, and a
   server that cannot determine the offer state MUST serve the
   conventional certificate.  The failure direction of
   misconfiguration is safe (a legacy client shown a BRAID certificate
   fails loudly on the unrecognized critical extension, never silently
   with weakened validation) and observable (such failures surface in
   the monitor-phase telemetry of Section 12.1 before enforcement is
   enabled).

   *Downgrade resistance.*  Negotiation creates a first-contact
   downgrade surface: an attacker holding a stolen conventional
   certificate for the domain could strip the braid_chain offer or
   simply answer with the conventional certificate.  Three graduated
   mitigations apply.  First, the owner publishes a DNSSEC-signed
   BRAID Policy record whose strict mode instructs BRAID-capable
   clients that the domain MUST present a BRAID certificate; a client
   that has validated such a record MUST reject a conventional
   certificate for that domain.  Second, domains MAY be enrolled in a
   preloaded BRAID-required set distributed with client software, in
   the manner of HSTS [RFC6797], protecting even first contact.
   Third, a client SHOULD cache a domain's observed BRAID policy for
   its stated lifetime.  The conventional certificate a BRAID operator
   holds is, by design, short-lived under prevailing root-program
   rules, so the residual downgrade exposure is bounded by the shorter
   of the policy-record lifetime and that certificate's own lifetime.

11.  Proof Transport: braid_chain and Reference-Based Caching

   A naive design that staples complete DNSSEC proof chains into every
   handshake inflates the server's first flight and burdens every
   connection with material that changes rarely.  BRAID transports
   proofs by reference, not by value.

   The Anchor is authorization state, not per-connection status.  A
   verifier checks whether the credential key used in the handshake is
   among the owner-authorized keys in a DNSSEC-authenticated RRset;
   nothing about that check requires contacting a third party per
   connection.  A client MUST NOT perform a per-connection query to a
   third-party status service as a prerequisite for strict Identity
   validation.  Cache-miss retrieval of proof material is permitted
   only when the retrieved object is self-authenticating by hash and
   when the retrieval policy does not reveal per-origin browsing
   behavior to an unrelated third party.

   *  The server sends compact identifiers --- a hash and, optionally,
      a URI --- for the validation material (the DNSSEC chain to the
      Anchor) via the braid_chain TLS extension, whose presence in the
      ClientHello also serves as the negotiation signal (Section 10).

   *  Clients maintain a cache of validated chains, keyed by hash.
      Because the referenced material changes rarely (under BRAID-Base,
      only on rotation or revocation), the steady-state handshake
      carries only the Delegated Credential and the compact
      references.

   *  Only on a cache miss does the client fetch the full chain ---
      preferentially from the server it is connecting to (which learns
      nothing it does not already know), or from a repository only
      over a privacy-preserving transport such as Oblivious HTTP
      [RFC9458] or via a batched, prefetched snapshot.  A client MUST
      NOT emit a per-connection identifying query to a third-party
      repository as a matter of course.

   Each reference is a cryptographic hash of the material it names.  A
   client MUST verify that fetched or cached content matches the
   referenced hash before relying on it; the URI, if present, is a
   convenience locator only and is never trusted on its own.  Caches
   keyed by hash are self-certifying and safely shareable across
   connections and origins.  In-band stapling of a full DNSSEC chain,
   in the style of the experimental [RFC9102], is supported only as an
   OPTIONAL fallback and is not a load-bearing dependency.

11.1.  Resource management and denial of service

   A reference cache is itself an attack surface.  Four properties
   bound it.  First, the number of references per handshake is
   derivable, not open-ended: a server presents at most one reference
   per required strand plus one for the policy record, and a client
   MUST reject a braid_chain extension exceeding that bound.  Second,
   admission is verification-gated: only material whose hash has been
   checked is stored, so unresolvable or malformed references occupy
   no cache space.  Third, eviction and allocation are the client's to
   govern: caches SHOULD use recency-based eviction with per-origin
   allocation limits.  Fourth, failure is negatively cached: a
   reference whose fetch fails SHOULD be recorded as unavailable for a
   bounded interval rather than triggering repeated fetches.  Fetched
   material MUST be size-bounded before parsing; the largest
   legitimate object, a DNSSEC chain, is a few kilobytes, so a limit
   of 16 KB is ample.

12.  Deployment

   Deployment is staged so that each stage delivers standalone value
   with no dependence on a later stage: no flag day, no stranded
   investment, and no adopter waiting on someone else.

12.1.  Monitor-only deployment

   A domain MAY publish a BRAID Anchor before any client enforces
   BRAID.  In monitor-only mode, third-party monitors and the domain
   operator compare the Delegated Credential keys observed at TLS
   endpoints with the keys authorized in the Anchor.  A mismatch does
   not affect client connections, but it is operationally useful: it
   detects unauthorized credential deployment, stale edge
   configuration, accidental key reuse, and a class of serving states
   not visible in Certificate Transparency --- CT shows what
   certificates exist, while an Anchor mismatch shows a certificate or
   credential serving traffic that the owner never authorized.

   Monitor-only deployment requires no new TLS extension, no new
   certificate profile, no browser change, and no root-program change:
   its entire mechanism is DNS publication plus out-of-band
   comparison.  The cost of starting is a DNS record and a monitoring
   job, nothing published is thrown away later, and the owner acquires
   and rehearses an owner-controlled de-authorization capability
   (rotating the Anchor and observing propagation) before any client
   depends on it.  It is therefore the first deployment target and the
   recommended initial implementation target.

12.2.  Managed strict enforcement

   Enterprises, financial institutions, and closed networks operate
   their own root programs and control both endpoints, so they are not
   gated on public root-program or browser decisions.  They can issue
   certificates carrying BRAIDBinding under private-use codepoints,
   enforce the Identity strand in managed clients under the Managed-
   Resolver mode of Section 9, and generate the fail-closed
   operational record that public-web standardization will ask for ---
   in exactly the regulated, high-assurance environments that want the
   strongest guarantees first.

12.3.  Public-Web enforcement

   Public CAs issue BRAID certificates at whatever maximum lifetime
   root programs then require, and clients ship validation behind a
   flag, progressing domains through monitor, staged, and strict
   policy modes as telemetry warrants, in the manner of DMARC's
   none/quarantine/reject ladder.  The one root-program ask is small
   and severable from lifetimes: permitting the single BRAIDBinding
   extension in leaf certificates requires a scoped certificate-
   profile ballot --- a permission-to-carry change, not a lifetime
   change.  A BRAID certificate at prevailing lifetimes is already
   strictly stronger than a conventional one: owner-controlled de-
   authorization, defense against stolen end-entity keys and CA
   compromise via the independent Anchor, and delegated name
   constraints for third-party serving.

   Graceful degradation MUST NOT satisfy a public-Web policy that
   requires the Identity strand in strict mode.  If the Identity
   strand is required and the client cannot authenticate the Anchor
   under the required trust mode, the connection fails.  A relying
   party MAY configure local enterprise exceptions outside the public-
   Web profile, but such exceptions are local policy and do not
   constitute BRAID strict validation.  This rule is deliberate:
   BRAID's central claim is fail-closed enforcement, and a mechanism
   that claims fail-closed while permitting soft-fail under
   operational pressure recreates the failure mode that discredited
   OCSP and must-staple.  Fail-closed is painful but honest; the
   staged policy ladder and the monitor telemetry exist precisely so
   that operators reach strict mode with evidence rather than hope.

12.4.  The freshness profile (BRAID-Agile)

   BRAID-Agile is an OPTIONAL enforcement profile, selected in
   BRAIDBinding or by relying-party policy, under which the verifier
   additionally enforces bounds on credential age and Anchor-entry
   validity (step 9 of Section 8).  High-assurance operators opt in
   to a strict freshness window --- as short as 24 hours, at most the
   [RFC9345] seven-day maximum --- accepting an automated key-rotation
   obligation in exchange for two bounded exposures: a stolen
   credential key becomes useless when its window lapses even if the
   owner never notices the theft, and an owner-initiated de-
   authorization is globally effective within one window even against
   caches that have not observed the Anchor change.

   BRAID-Agile changes the operational posture, not the security
   model: the two-control property of Section 4.1 is identical in both
   profiles.  An operator SHOULD choose BRAID-Agile only where its
   rotation discipline meets the bar of Section 13.2.

13.  Operational Considerations

13.1.  Steady-state operations under BRAID-Base

   Under BRAID-Base the steady state involves no DNS-side automation
   and no CA-facing automation attributable to BRAID:

   *  The Anchor RRset is written once and changed only for rotation
      or revocation.  The DNS publication path is exercised on the
      owner's initiative, not on a renewal clock.

   *  The credential key pair MAY be long-lived.  Because [RFC9345]
      caps the credential object at seven days, the operator re-signs
      a fresh credential over the same key pair on a local schedule.
      This is the only recurring operation, and it is local and self-
      contained: it requires no CA interaction, no domain-control
      validation, no rate-limit exposure, no CT logging event, and no
      DNS write.  It is performed with the end-entity key the operator
      already holds, can be pre-computed arbitrarily far in advance
      within the seven-day bound, and its failure is visible to the
      operator's own monitoring (the server simply has no fresh
      credential to present) rather than manifesting first as a
      client-side validation failure against a third party.

   *  The conventional certificate for non-BRAID clients continues to
      renew under the operator's existing ACME automation [RFC8555],
      unchanged; BRAID neither adds to nor removes that obligation
      (Section 2).

   The contrast with must-staple is the location of the failure
   domain: must-staple's fresh evidence came from a third party the
   operator did not control (the CA's OCSP responder), whose outages
   forced soft-fail; BRAID-Base's only recurring artifact is minted by
   the operator, on the operator's hardware, from the operator's key.
   More generally, the recurring operations BRAID-Base does involve
   --- local credential re-signing and, on the owner's initiative,
   Anchor updates --- are owner-scheduled operations against
   infrastructure the owner controls, whereas each certificate
   issuance event is a transaction with an external party, subject to
   that party's API availability, rate limits, validation-method
   requirements, and commercial terms.  Reducing the frequency of the
   latter class of operation, where a relying-party ecosystem permits
   it, is therefore an operational simplification in kind and not only
   in count.

13.2.  Rotation at scale under BRAID-Agile

   The freshness window is only as safe as the rotation discipline
   behind it, and large anycast or multi-region fleets face real
   propagation lag.  Three practices, borrowed from DNSSEC key-
   rollover operations, bound the fail-closed surface: the next
   window's credential key SHOULD be pre-published in the Anchor RRset
   before any server presents it, so propagation completes while the
   previous key is still serving; the Anchor RRset's TTL SHOULD be a
   small fraction of the freshness window; and fleets SHOULD stagger
   credential validity across regions rather than expiring globally at
   one instant.  With pre-publication in place, the steady state
   always has at least two authorized keys in the Anchor --- current
   and next --- and a rotation failure gives a full window of margin
   before any client fails closed.

13.3.  Revocation procedure

   To de-authorize a credential key (stolen, retired, or belonging to
   a de-provisioned edge), the owner removes its Anchor entry; service
   continues uninterrupted on the remaining authorized keys.  To
   revoke the certificate's usability entirely --- for example, upon
   end-entity key compromise --- the owner empties or withdraws the
   Anchor RRset; BRAID-enforcing clients then fail the Identity strand
   for that certificate.  Propagation is bounded by DNS TTLs and, for
   clients holding cached proof chains, by the cached material's
   DNSSEC signature validity; under BRAID-Agile it is additionally
   bounded by the freshness window.  Owners requiring immediate de-
   authorization SHOULD both update the Anchor and cease serving the
   excluded credential.  Conventional CA revocation of the underlying
   certificate remains available and SHOULD also be performed; the
   mechanisms are complementary.

14.  Certificate Validity Periods

   This document does not define maximum validity periods for publicly
   trusted TLS certificates.  Those periods are set by root-program
   and CA/Browser Forum policy, not by the IETF.  BRAID's protocol
   claim is narrower: when the Identity strand is enforced, the
   effective exposure of a stolen end-entity key is bounded by the
   attacker's inability to authorize a new credential key in the
   owner's Anchor, and the exposure of a stolen Delegated Credential
   key is bounded by the applicable credential and Anchor-entry
   validity intervals.

   A future root-program profile could use deployment evidence from
   BRAID strict Identity validation to justify a distinct validity
   class for certificates carrying an enforced Identity strand.  Such
   a profile is out of scope for this document.  Implementations of
   this document MUST remain useful at prevailing public-Web
   certificate lifetimes, and the deployment stages of Section 12 are
   sequenced so that every stage stands on its own security value with
   no dependence on any lifetime change.

15.  Security Considerations

   *The two-control property and its collapse conditions.*  The
   property of Section 4.1 governs the stolen-key analysis: theft of
   the end-entity private key alone does not permit impersonation of a
   BRAID-strict domain, and theft of a credential key alone is cured
   by one Anchor update (and bounded by the window under BRAID-Agile).
   The property collapses if the two controls are co-resident: a DNS
   update credential stored on the TLS-terminating host, or a DNS
   provider account protected by credentials kept alongside the server
   keys, reduces two controls to one.  Section 4.1's separation
   guidance is therefore part of the deployment, not an optimization.
   The property is also only as strong as the owner's DNS publication
   path: registrar-account takeover, weak registrar authentication, or
   a compromised DNS operator substitutes for zone compromise, and
   operators SHOULD apply registry/registrar locks and strong
   authentication on that path.

   *Downgrade and stripping.*  The validity requirement is carried in
   a critical certificate extension, reinforced by the TLS Feature
   extension [RFC7633] and, for enrolled domains, by the policy record
   and preloaded set of Section 10.  An on-path attacker can withhold
   BRAID material but cannot make a BRAID certificate validate without
   it.  The first-contact downgrade surface and its graduated
   mitigations are analyzed in Section 10; operators for whom the
   residue is unacceptable use preloading.

   *DNSSEC trust modes.*  The modal design of Section 9 exists because
   the security of the Anchor check is exactly the security of the
   DNSSEC validation behind it.  In Public-Web strict mode the
   verifier's conclusions rest on self-authenticating material; in
   Managed-Resolver mode they rest additionally on the administrative
   trust placed in the resolver and the authenticated channel to it.
   A verifier MUST NOT silently substitute the weaker mode where the
   stronger is required.

   *Fail-closed availability.*  Strict enforcement couples
   availability to the owner's DNS publication path and DNSSEC
   signing.  This is a deliberate reassignment of risk to the party
   with the power and incentive to manage it, and it is bounded
   operationally rather than cryptographically: monitor-mode telemetry
   before enforcement, pre-publication and TTL discipline
   (Section 13.2), and the prohibition on degradation for strict
   Identity (Section 12.3), which prevents the soft-fail regression
   that discredited prior mechanisms.

   *Delegated Credential considerations.*  The cross-protocol and
   clock-skew considerations of [RFC9345] apply unchanged.  Clock skew
   additionally bounds how quickly a de-authorization takes effect;
   Section 13.3 applies.

   *Anchor availability as a target.*  An adversary who can suppress a
   client's ability to authenticate the Anchor (for example, by
   blocking proof fetches) achieves denial of service against strict-
   mode connections, not impersonation.  The negative-caching and
   size-bounding rules of Section 11.1 bound the amplification of such
   attacks; the reassignment of this availability risk to the owner is
   discussed above.

16.  Privacy Considerations

   BRAID introduces no per-connection query to any third party.  The
   Anchor check is performed against cached, stapled, fetched-by-hash,
   or distribution-provided material (Section 11); a client MUST NOT
   emit a per-connection identifying query to a third-party repository
   as a matter of course, and repository access, where used, is
   restricted to privacy-preserving transports such as Oblivious HTTP
   [RFC9458] or batched snapshots.  Where a client queries a resolver
   for the Anchor name (Managed-Resolver mode), the query is directed
   to the same resolver that resolved the server's name and reveals
   nothing that name resolution did not already reveal.  BRAID
   therefore does not reproduce the OCSP privacy leak.

17.  Implementation Status

   _This section records the status of known implementations per
   [RFC7942].  RFC Editor: please remove this section before
   publication._

   No complete implementation of this document exists at the time of
   writing; the monitor-only mode of Section 12.1 requires none.  The
   building blocks are individually implemented and deployed:
   Delegated Credentials [RFC9345] ship in a major browser and in
   widely used TLS libraries and are deployed by large CDN operators;
   DNSSEC validation is standard in major recursive resolvers;
   authenticated browser-distributed data sets (HSTS preload, CRLite-
   style revocation snapshots) are in production; and ACME automation
   [RFC8555] is the dominant issuance path.  A monitor-only
   implementation --- an Anchor publisher plus a scanner comparing
   observed Delegated Credential keys against Anchor membership,
   exercising the negative test vectors of Appendix A --- is the
   authors' immediate target, and reports of independent
   implementations are solicited for recording here.

18.  IANA Considerations

   This document requests:

   *  a TLS ExtensionType for braid_chain in the TLS ExtensionType
      Values registry;

   *  a single object identifier for the BRAIDBinding certificate
      extension, within which all BRAID parameters are serialized, so
      that no parallel certificate-extension identifiers are required;

   *  a DNS RRTYPE for the BRAID Anchor record and a record format for
      the BRAID Policy record; and

   *  a registry for BRAID policy modes.

   Several of these registrations require coordination beyond the TLS
   Working Group, and the work is split accordingly (Appendix B): the
   Anchor and Policy RRTYPEs require DNS expert review and are
   expected to involve the DNSOP community; the BRAIDBinding ASN.1
   warrants LAMPS review; and the extension profiles of Section 19
   would involve SIDROPS and TRANS respectively if progressed.  Early
   review by those groups is anticipated.

19.  Future Extension Profiles: Routing and Witness

   The -00 revision of this document defined two further strands as
   part of the core architecture.  This revision retains them as named
   extension profiles, described here to show architectural
   compatibility; they are NOT required for BRAID-Base validation, are
   not prerequisites for any deployment stage of Section 12, and their
   full specifications are deferred to companion documents
   (Appendix B).  Strand indicators for both are reserved in
   BRAIDBinding (Section 6.2).

   *BRAID-Routed (Routing strand).*  An assertion, checked against
   routing data validated by RPKI relying-party infrastructure
   [RFC6480], that the connection originates from an authorized
   origin AS, optionally narrowed to bound prefixes or addresses.  Its
   distinct contribution is protection at use time against BGP-hijack
   interception --- issuance-time hijacks being primarily addressed by
   the CA/Browser Forum's Multi-Perspective Issuance Corroboration,
   and independently defeated by the Identity strand, since the
   owner's Anchor never authorized the resulting certificate's
   credentials.  A prerequisite is a validated-origin query by which a
   stub can ask its resolver whether an (address, origin AS) pair is
   RPKI-valid; that query does not exist today, belongs to the DNSOP/
   SIDROPS communities, and is named as new work rather than assumed.

   *BRAID-Witnessed (Witness strand).*  A counter-signature over the
   certificate by an appointed third party, operationally independent
   of both the issuing CA and the subscriber, anchored in a public
   log, encoded in the signed-timestamp format of [RFC6962]/[RFC9162].
   With optional witnessed freshness, the witness's renewal decision
   becomes a revocation lever that survives the simultaneous
   compromise of both Identity-strand controls --- the one scenario
   Section 2 places out of scope for BRAID-Base --- at the cost of a
   sustained availability dependency on the witness.  Unlike the
   Routing profile, the Witness profile has no missing protocol
   dependency: its formats and log infrastructure exist today, and it
   is deployable now in the managed environments of Section 12.2.  Its
   full specification --- the co-signing format, witnessed freshness,
   witness independence and appointment-terms requirements, and
   private-use gating --- is planned as a near-term companion
   document.

   Both profiles compose with, and depend on, the Identity strand
   defined by this document; neither modifies its validation
   algorithm.

Appendix A.  Negative Test Vectors

   A specification is only as trustworthy as its fail cases.  The
   following vectors are normative fail cases for the algorithm of
   Section 8 and are the recommended initial test suite for monitor
   and verifier implementations.

   A.1.  Stolen EE key, attacker-minted credential.  Certificate
         validates; Delegated Credential validates under the EE key;
         the credential public key is absent from the Anchor.
         Result: FAIL Identity (step 8).

   A.2.  Authorized credential key, wrong DNS-ID.  The credential key
         appears in the Anchor entry covering static.example.com; the
         client validates auth.example.com.  Result: FAIL unless the
         entry's covered DNS-ID set includes the validated name
         (steps 7-8).

   A.3.  Unsigned Anchor.  The RRset exists but DNSSEC authentication
         fails or is absent under the required trust mode.  Result:
         FAIL Identity (step 6).

   A.4.  Stale credential key.  The credential key was previously
         authorized but its entry has been removed from the Anchor.
         Result: FAIL once cached material expires per TTL and
         signature-validity rules; a monitor reports the transition
         window (Section 13.3).

   A.5.  Resolver-only AD bit in Public-Web strict mode.  The
         resolver asserts AD but no verifiable proof material is
         available.  Result: FAIL Public-Web strict mode (Section 9).

   A.6.  Degradation attempt against strict Identity.  Identity proof
         is unavailable; the WebPKI certificate otherwise validates.
         Result: FAIL; degradation is prohibited for strict Identity
         (Section 12.3).

   A.7.  TLSA usage 1 control case.  A TLSA usage 1 record
         authenticates the EE key; the attacker holds the stolen EE
         key.  Expected contrast: TLSA validation does not distinguish
         attacker use; BRAID fails absent Anchor membership
         (Section 5).

   A.8.  Multi-SAN mismatch.  The Anchor authorizes a credential key
         for one SAN of a shared certificate; the client connects to a
         different SAN.  Result: FAIL unless covered (Section 7.2).

   A.9.  Anchor pointer outside the identity boundary.  BRAIDBinding
         names an external Anchor without a DNSSEC-authenticated
         indirection record at the default Anchor name.  Result: FAIL
         (Section 7.1).

   A.10. Malformed wildcard constraint.  A delegated name constraint
         uses a partial-label wildcard or an invalid A-label.  Result:
         FAIL; malformed names are rejected, not repaired
         (Section 6.3).

Appendix C.  Change Log

   _RFC Editor: please remove this section before publication._

   Changes from draft-davey-tls-braid-00, incorporating mailing-list
   review:

   *  Recentered the document on the two-control property as a
      consequence of Anchor membership rather than freshness: the
      property is now stated (Section 4.1) and titled as independent
      of rotation cadence, the abstract and title were rewritten
      accordingly, and freshness was moved to the optional BRAID-Agile
      enforcement profile (Section 12.4).  This resolves the
      objection that BRAID relocated, rather than reduced, the
      automation burden of short-lived credentials: under BRAID-Base
      the Anchor and credential key are static, and the only recurring
      operation is local credential re-signing with no external
      dependency (Section 13.1).

   *  Added Section 5, a precise comparison with DANE TLSA usage 1
      (and usage 3), stating exactly why a static TLSA binding does
      not stop a stolen end-entity key while Anchor membership does,
      analyzing the differing revocation-by-withdrawal semantics, and
      addressing the deployment-inference argument.

   *  Replaced the implicit resolver trust story with two explicit
      DNSSEC validation modes (Section 9): Public-Web strict (self-
      authenticating proof material only; unauthenticated AD bits
      insufficient) and Managed-Resolver (administratively trusted
      validating resolver over an authenticated channel), with a
      prohibition on silent substitution.

   *  Prohibited graceful degradation for strict Identity
      (Section 12.3), removing the -00 degradation option for the
      Identity strand entirely; degradation semantics, if any, now
      belong only to future extension strands.

   *  Made Anchor placement deterministic (Section 7): default
      _braid.<DNS-ID> location, authenticated-indirection rule for
      external Anchors, explicit exclusion of CNAME chasing as an
      authorization mechanism, and multi-SAN/wildcard coverage rules.

   *  Replaced the normative five-year validity claim with
      Section 14: validity periods are root-program policy, out of
      scope; implementations must be useful at prevailing lifetimes;
      any extended-validity class is future non-IETF policy contingent
      on deployment evidence.

   *  Moved the Routing and Witness strands to Future Extension
      Profiles (Section 19) with their specifications deferred to
      companion documents; reserved their BRAIDBinding indicators;
      reduced the IANA footprint accordingly.  The two profiles have
      opposite readiness: the Witness profile has no missing protocol
      dependency and its companion document is near-term, while the
      Routing profile waits on the validated-origin query.

   *  Added the normative validation algorithm (Section 8), the
      negative test vectors (Appendix A), the monitor-only deployment
      section (Section 12.1), the Non-Goals section (Section 2), and
      the IETF work-split roadmap (Appendix B).

   *  Retained from -00 without substantive change: negotiation and
      certificate selection with operational isolation and downgrade
      resistance (Section 10), reference-based proof transport with
      DoS bounds and privacy rules (Section 11), delegated name
      constraints with A-label matching rules (Section 6.3), and the
      rotation-at-scale guidance, now scoped to BRAID-Agile
      (Section 13.2).
